# Why Your Dog Deserves a Fantasy Portrait

Think about what your dog actually does.

They wake up every morning and their entire day is oriented around you. When you come home — whether you've been gone five minutes or five hours — they greet you like you've returned from war. When you're sick, they lie next to you without being asked. When you're sad, they come to you.

They don't do any of this for money, status, or recognition. They do it because you are their person and that is simply what they do.

If that's not the behavior of a fantasy hero, I don't know what is.

## The Gap Between What They Are and How We See Them

We have thousands of photos of our dogs. On the couch. At the park. Mid-zoomies. Waiting for their food. These photos are wonderful, but they capture the ordinary version of an extraordinary creature.

A fantasy portrait changes the framing. It takes your dog's face — the exact face you know — and places it in a context that matches who they actually are. The loyal dog who would do anything for you, rendered as the Paladin they've always been. The chaotic gremlin who destroys things in the most endearing way, rendered as the Barbarian they clearly are.

It's not that the portrait lies. It's that it tells a different kind of truth.

## Dogs Have Always Been Heroes

The relationship between humans and dogs is at least 15,000 years old. Dogs weren't just companions — they were hunters, guards, herders, search and rescue workers. They ran into danger. They protected the vulnerable. They worked.

In every culture that has told stories, dogs appear as loyal companions to heroes. Odysseus's dog Argos waited 20 years for his master to return. Medieval knights rode into battle with their dogs beside them. Explorers brought dogs to the ends of the earth.

Your dog is part of that lineage. They carry that history in their instincts — the alert at a strange sound, the way they position themselves between you and something unknown, the impulse to stay close.

## What a Portrait Does

A fantasy portrait doesn't change your dog. It reveals something that was already there.

When you see your dog rendered as a Cleric in white and gold vestments, holding a glowing mace, something clicks. *Yes. That's actually them.* The patience. The devotion. The way they always seem to know when someone needs comfort.

When you see the Barbarian portrait — your chaotic, joyful, boundless dog rendered in battle-worn fur armor, greataxe raised — you laugh because it's *correct*. That's the energy. That's who they are.

The portrait is a gift — to yourself, to your dog (who won't understand it but will feel the joy in the room when you look at it), and to anyone you share it with.
